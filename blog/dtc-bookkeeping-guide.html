If you're running a DTC brand on Shopify, your bookkeeping is fundamentally different from a normal business. You're not selling to other businesses on net-30 terms. You're processing hundreds of small consumer payments through multiple processors, dealing with refunds, chargebacks, COGS that include shipping and packaging, and sales tax across multiple states.

Standard bookkeeping advice doesn't cover this. Most accountants don't understand it either — unless they specifically work with ecommerce brands.

This guide covers everything you need to know to keep accurate books for your Shopify DTC brand. Whether you do it yourself or hire someone, this is the playbook.

Why DTC Bookkeeping Is Different

A traditional business sells products or services, sends an invoice, and gets paid 30-60 days later. Simple. One revenue line. One payment. Easy to track.

A DTC brand on Shopify does something completely different:

  • Hundreds or thousands of small transactions per month (not dozens of invoices)
  • Multiple payment processors (Shopify Payments, PayPal, Apple Pay, Affirm)
  • Revenue that arrives net of fees (not gross)
  • Refunds and chargebacks that deduct from future payouts (not separate transactions)
  • COGS that include product cost + shipping + packaging + fulfillment fees
  • Sales tax collection across multiple states with different rates and rules
  • Inventory that needs to be tracked at cost (not just units)

If you treat your DTC brand like a traditional business in your books, your numbers will be wrong. Your profit margins will be wrong. Your tax bill will be wrong. And you won't know any of it until it's too late.

The key principle: gross sales go in revenue, fees go in expenses, refunds are contra-revenue. Never net them all together.

COGS: The Number Most Founders Get Wrong

Cost of Goods Sold (COGS) is the single most important number for a DTC brand — and the one most founders calculate incorrectly.

COGS is not just the cost of your product. It's everything directly required to get the product to the customer's door:

COGS =
Product manufacturing/purchase cost
+ Inbound freight (getting inventory to warehouse)
+ Packaging materials (box, mailer, insert, tape)
+ Fulfillment/picking fees (3PL or in-house labor)
+ Outbound shipping (what you pay UPS/FedEx/USPS)
− Shipping revenue (what the customer paid for shipping)

Example: You sell a skincare product for $45.

  • Product cost: $8
  • Packaging: $1.50
  • Fulfillment fee: $3.50
  • Outbound shipping: $6
  • Customer paid shipping: $5
  • COGS: $14
  • Gross margin: 69% ($31 gross profit / $45 revenue)

Most founders think their margin is 82% ($45 - $8 = $37 / $45). That's wrong. The real margin is 69%. That 13-point difference is massive at scale.

Recording Shopify Fees as Expenses

Every payment processor charges fees. For a DTC brand doing $500K/year, transaction fees alone can be $15,000-20,000/year. These are real expenses that need to be tracked.

Here's how to record them:

  • Shopify Payments fees: 2.9% + $0.30 per transaction (Basic plan). Record as "Payment Processing Fees" under operating expenses.
  • PayPal fees: 2.99% + $0.49 per transaction. Same category.
  • Shopify subscription: $39/mo (Basic) or $105/mo (Shopify). Record as "Software Subscription."
  • App transaction fees: Recharge, Afterpay, etc. charge per-transaction fees. Record as "Payment Processing Fees" or a separate "App Fees" category.
  • Currency conversion fees: 1-2% on international sales. Record as "FX Fees" or "Bank Fees."

Pro tip: In your Shopify payout export, fees are listed as separate line items. Export monthly and categorize them. It takes 15 minutes and gives you an accurate picture of your true processing costs.

Returns, Refunds, and Chargebacks

Returns are a cost of doing business in DTC. Average return rates by category:

  • Apparel: 20-30%
  • Skincare/beauty: 3-5%
  • Supplements: 2-4%
  • Home goods: 5-10%

Here's how to handle each in your books:

Refunds: Record as contra-revenue (negative revenue), not as an expense. This keeps your gross sales accurate and shows the true net revenue.

Chargebacks: Record the chargeback amount as contra-revenue AND the chargeback fee ($15-25 per dispute) as an expense. Chargebacks are expensive — track them separately so you can see the real cost.

Return shipping costs: If you offer free returns, the return shipping cost is a COGS expense. If the customer pays return shipping, it doesn't affect your books.

Sales Tax: The Silent Killer

Sales tax is the thing that keeps DTC founders up at night — and for good reason. After the 2018 South Dakota v. Wayfair Supreme Court decision, you're required to collect and remit sales tax in every state where you have "nexus" (economic presence).

For most DTC brands, nexus is triggered when you hit a threshold — usually $100,000 in sales or 200 transactions in a state. Once you cross that threshold, you're legally required to:

  1. Register for a sales tax permit in that state
  2. Collect sales tax from customers in that state
  3. File and remit the tax (monthly, quarterly, or annually)

Shopify collects the tax for you. But remitting it is your responsibility. If you don't file, you owe the tax plus penalties and interest — even if you collected it from customers.

How to stay compliant:

  • Track your sales by state monthly (Shopify reports show this)
  • Register in states where you cross the threshold
  • File on time, even if you owe $0 (most states require a "zero return")
  • Use a service like Avalara or TaxJar to automate filing ($20-50/month)

Monthly Bookkeeping Checklist for DTC Brands

Here's your monthly routine. Takes 2-3 hours if you stay on top of it:

  1. Reconcile Shopify payouts with bank deposits (see our payout reconciliation guide)
  2. Categorize all transactions in QuickBooks/Xero (or your spreadsheet)
  3. Record COGS — product costs, shipping, packaging, fulfillment
  4. Review sales tax — check if you've crossed thresholds in new states
  5. Record ad spend — Meta, Google, TikTok, influencer payments
  6. Review your P&L — are margins where they should be?
  7. Check inventory — reconcile physical count with book count
  8. Save receipts — for any expenses paid outside of Shopify
