You check your Shopify dashboard. It says you made $50,000 last month. You check your bank account. $46,800 landed. Where did $3,200 go?

If this sounds familiar, you're not alone. This is the single most common bookkeeping problem for Shopify merchants — and almost nobody explains it properly.

The gap isn't a mistake. It's not fraud. It's the difference between gross sales and net payouts, and understanding that difference is the foundation of accurate books for any DTC brand.

Why Shopify Payouts Never Match Your Bank Deposits

Here's the fundamental problem: Shopify doesn't transfer your revenue to your bank. It transfers your net payout — which is your revenue minus a stack of deductions that most founders don't fully understand.

When a customer pays you $100 for a product, here's what actually happens:

$100.00 — Customer payment (gross sale)
− $2.90 — Shopify Payments processing fee (2.9% + $0.30)
− $0.00 — Shopify subscription fee (not per-transaction)
− $1.50 — Refund reserve holdback (if applicable)
− $0.80 — App transaction fees (Recharge, etc.)
− $0.00 — Currency conversion (if domestic)
= $94.80 — What actually hits your bank

That $5.20 gap on a single $100 transaction? Multiply that by thousands of orders per month, and you're looking at thousands of dollars that "disappeared" from your revenue.

Shopify's dashboard shows you the $100. Your bank shows you $94.80. Your books need to account for both — and explain the difference.

What's Actually Inside a Shopify Payout

A single Shopify payout can contain dozens of different transaction types. Here's the breakdown:

Transaction TypeWhat It IsImpact
Order paymentsCustomer charges (gross)Increases payout
RefundsCustomer refunds processedDecreases payout
Processing feesShopify Payments / PayPal / Stripe feesDecreases payout
ChargebacksDisputed chargesDecreases payout
AdjustmentsShopify corrections, shipping label adjustmentsVaries
Reserve holdbacksRisk reserve (new/high-risk stores)Decreases payout
Currency conversionFX fees for international salesDecreases payout

The critical insight: your payout is not your revenue. It's your revenue minus all of the above. If you log the payout amount as revenue in your books, your revenue is understated and your margins are wrong.

Step-by-Step: How to Reconcile Shopify Payouts

Here's the exact process to reconcile your Shopify payouts with your bank deposits. Do this monthly.

Step 1: Export Your Shopify Payouts

In Shopify Admin → Payments → Payouts → Export. Select the date range for the month you're reconciling. You'll get a CSV with every payout transaction.

Step 2: Export Your Bank Statement

Log into your bank. Download the statement for the same date range. You're looking for the Shopify deposit transactions.

Step 3: Match Payouts to Deposits

Each Shopify payout has a unique ID and an expected deposit date. Match each payout to the corresponding bank deposit. In most cases, they should match exactly (minus timing differences).

Timing note: Shopify payouts typically take 2-3 business days to land in your bank. A payout dated March 31 might not appear in your bank until April 3. This is normal. Account for the lag.

Step 4: Break Down Each Payout

For each payout, separate the components:

  • Gross sales (total order value)
  • Refunds (money returned to customers)
  • Fees (processing fees, app fees)
  • Net payout (what hit your bank)

Step 5: Record in Your Books

Record the gross sales as revenue, not the net payout. Record the fees as expenses. This gives you an accurate picture of your actual revenue and your true profit margin.

Step 6: Investigate Discrepancies

If a payout doesn't match the bank deposit, check for:

  • Currency conversion differences (Shopify converts at their rate, not the mid-market rate)
  • Reserve holdbacks (new stores often have 5-10% held back)
  • Chargebacks that were deducted after the payout was calculated
  • Bank processing fees (some banks charge for incoming wires)

5 Common Mistakes That Break Your Books

Mistake #1: Logging net payouts as revenue. This is the most common error. If Shopify pays you $47,000, that's not your revenue — it's your revenue minus fees. Your actual revenue might be $50,000+. If you log $47K, you're understating revenue by 6%.

Mistake #2: Ignoring refund timing. A refund processed in March might be deducted from your April payout. If you don't track this, your March books show revenue you didn't actually keep.

Mistake #3: Mixing payment processors. If you use both Shopify Payments and PayPal, you'll have two separate payout streams with different fee structures. Reconcile each one separately.

Mistake #4: Forgetting app transaction fees. Apps like Recharge, ReBold, and others charge per-transaction fees that are deducted from your payouts. These are real expenses that need to be tracked.

Mistake #5: Not reconciling monthly. The longer you wait, the harder it becomes. A month's worth of payouts takes 30-60 minutes to reconcile. A year's worth takes days.

Multi-Currency Payouts (The Hidden Killer)

If you sell internationally, multi-currency payouts add another layer of complexity. Here's what happens:

A customer in the UK pays £80 for your product. Shopify converts that to USD at their exchange rate (not the mid-market rate). The conversion fee is typically 1-2%. So your £80 sale might become $97.50 instead of the $100 you'd get at the real exchange rate.

That $2.50 difference per transaction adds up fast. On $100K in international sales, you could lose $2,000-3,000/year just on currency conversion spreads.

How to track this: In your Shopify payout export, look for the "currency_conversion_fee" line items. Record these as a separate expense category so you can see exactly how much you're losing to FX.
